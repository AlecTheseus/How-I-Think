# The Inverted Civilization: The True Engine of Human Progress Has Never Been "Science"

## I. Introduction: An Answer We Take for Granted, But That May Be Wrong

We have been raised on a seemingly self-evident narrative about the origin of human knowledge: geniuses conceive principles in their minds, from which theories are born; engineers translate these theories into technologies; and finally, factories and workers turn technologies into products. This is a linear chain that runs from "high" to "low," from "mind" to "hands," from "heaven" to "earth."

The narrative is so seamless that few ever question it. But it is very likely upside down.

The knowledge that drives human progress comes first from a more humble, more clumsy, and more honest process: **"doing."** In their practical attempts to change the world—first to survive, then to expand production—people encounter concrete problems, engage in trial and error, accumulate experience, distill local rules from it, and only then systematize these rules into theory. Theory is not a prerequisite for practice, but a summary and codification of practical experience.

This is not philosophical speculation, but a fact that history repeatedly verifies. If we are willing to set aside our reverence for "pure thought" and take a serious look at the British Industrial Revolution, China's reform and opening-up, the daily practice of software engineering, and the vastly different fates of Edison and Tesla, we will discover a long-inverted truth: **the engine of knowledge is not in the clouds, but in the soil.**


## II. The Structural Bias of Academia: Worshiping "Science," Dismissing "Technology"

If there is one place that should best understand the nature of knowledge, it should be academia. Yet it is precisely here that the misunderstanding of knowledge's origins is most deeply entrenched.

In today's academic evaluation system, there exists a deeply ingrained hierarchy. A paper revealing "new principles" or "new mechanisms" can be published in top journals like *Nature* or *Science*, bringing its author faculty positions, funding, and lifelong reputation. But another kind of work—say, improving the yield of a material's preparation from 70% to 95%, or finding a process route that cuts production costs in half—no matter how intellectually dense or how great its practical contribution to industry, is often categorized as an "engineering problem," dismissed as "mundane applied labor," and struggles to enter the core vision of academic evaluation.

This bias is not accidental; it has deep philosophical roots. Since Plato, the Western epistemological tradition has placed "reason" above "experience," considering the former more elevated, more pure, and closer to truth, while the latter is merely a shadow of a shadow. Within this framework, "thought" is naturally superior to "hands," "universal principles" naturally superior to "specific crafts," and "discovery" naturally superior to "making."

What are the consequences? Knowledge production is systematically narrowed into "paper production," while the silent, dense cognitive labor on production lines, in workshops, and during debugging processes is chronically undervalued or even erased. A scholar who can write elegant equations is hailed as a genius, while an engineer who can turn those equations into reproducible, low-cost, mass-produced products often finds their name at the very end of the author list.

This is a dangerous structural imbalance—it creates a bubble of "papers without products" while continuously reinforcing a false belief: **that truly valuable knowledge comes from the mind, not from the hands.**


## III. Two "Reversed" Modernizations: The British Industrial Revolution and China's Reform and Opening-up

If theory were truly the source of practice, then we should see: scientific revolution first, industrial revolution second; principle breakthroughs first, production leaps second. But history tells the opposite story.

### (I) The British Industrial Revolution: Not Newton's Apple, but the Blacksmith's Hands

The popular textbook narrative goes like this: Galileo and Newton established classical mechanics; Watt improved the steam engine based on this; the steam engine drove factories; factories drove the Industrial Revolution. This chain appears flawless—there's just one problem: it isn't true.

Watt's steam engine was indeed great, but more than half a century before him, a blacksmith named Newcomen had already built a working steam engine to pump water from deep mines in Cornwall. Newcomen didn't understand Newtonian mechanics; he just tried again and again to see how he could make that clunky machine pump a little faster.

Hargreaves, inventor of the spinning jenny, was a weaver and carpenter; Arkwright, inventor of the water frame, was a barber; Crompton, inventor of the mule, was a textile worker. Bessemer, who improved steelmaking, was not a scientist but an inventor and engineer—he even got the principle behind his "blowing air to burn off impurities" wrong (he thought blowing air raised the temperature, when in fact it was oxygen reacting with carbon that released heat)—but this didn't prevent his process from succeeding and revolutionizing the steel industry.

What were the scholars at Oxford and Cambridge doing at the time? They had almost no direct connection to the Industrial Revolution. The knowledge producers of the Industrial Revolution were those with oil-stained hands. Their driving force was not "exploring the mysteries of nature," but "making mine pumping cheaper," "making spinning faster and more productive," "making steel less costly." They got it right first; it was much later that theorists caught up to explain *why* they got it right.

The narrative of "scientific revolution driving industrial revolution" is a classic case of "history written by the victors"—retrospectively granting glory to the respectable figures while sweeping the true intellectual labor in the workshops into the dustbin of history.

### (II) China's Reform and Opening-up: "Do" First, R&D Comes Later

If the "production first, theory later" of the British Industrial Revolution was a spontaneous historical occurrence, then China's path resembles more a large-scale "unintentionally correct choice."

When reform and opening-up began in 1978, China's core goal was not "R&D," but the simplest of words: "production." Earning foreign exchange, absorbing employment, and solving food and clothing—these were the real drivers. The early policy logic was "three processing and one compensation" (processing with supplied materials, processing with supplied samples, processing with supplied parts, and compensation trade), joint ventures, and joining the global division of labor. The keyword was "manufacturing," not "innovation."

The timeline of policies over the years corroborates this:

- The **"Reinvigorating China through Science and Education"** strategy was proposed in **1995**—by which time reform and opening-up had already been underway for 17 years, and China was already a world factory of considerable scale.
- **"Indigenous Innovation"** was first explicitly written into national strategy in **2006**—the *National Medium- and Long-Term Plan for Science and Technology Development (2006–2020)* put forward the guiding principle of "indigenous innovation, leapfrogging in key areas, supporting development, and leading the future." By this time, China's manufacturing system had largely taken shape.
- The **"Innovation-Driven Development Strategy"** was elevated to a core position after the 18th National Congress in **2012**.
- Systematic discourse on **"New Quality Productive Forces"** emerged in **2023–2024**—this was a summary and elevation of existing practice, not a prior blueprint.

What does this mean? It means China did not first figure out "we need to pursue scientific and technological innovation" and then start acting. Quite the opposite: **It was only after China had become the world's largest producer, and encountered real bottlenecks in production—equipment dependence on imports, key materials being choked off—that it was forced to start catching up on the "theory" front.**

The photovoltaic industry is a typical example. China did not invent the principles of photovoltaics, but by scaling production to over 80% of global capacity, it overcame a series of process challenges in production—such as the hydrogenation of polysilicon and diamond wire cutting—and in turn gained control over core technologies and standards. Lithium batteries, high-speed rail, and OLEDs followed the same path: first achieve production through scale effects, accumulate experience in production, overcome bottlenecks, and then master core technologies in reverse.

Mainstream narratives often characterize this path as "catch-up through imitation" or a "failed market-for-technology" approach, but a more accurate description might be **"production forcing science."** Yet because this path does not fit the "theory first" script, it has long been undervalued and even misinterpreted as "lacking innovation."

### (III) The Needham Question: A Question That Was Wrong from the Start

"Why did China not produce modern science?"—the Needham Question has troubled Chinese intellectual circles for decades, with countless attempts to answer it. But a more fundamental question is: Is the question itself valid?

Its implicit premise is that Western modern mathematical-experimental science is the only standard form of human knowledge. By this standard, indeed China did not have it. But this conclusion is *defined*, not *discovered*—it defines "science" in terms of "the West," then asserts that China lacks it. This is a tautology, not a historical judgment.

The historical fact is that for a very long time, China possessed the world's most advanced technological systems—metallurgy, textiles, navigation, papermaking, printing—and behind these technologies lay a self-consistent, highly systematized body of practical knowledge. Movable type printing, the water-powered spinning wheel, and watertight bulkhead shipbuilding technology of the Song Dynasty were not sudden flashes of genius from some individual, but systematic knowledge accumulated over long periods of production practice.

Perhaps the question truly worth asking is not "Why didn't China...," but: **"Is the Western mode of knowledge production—which places theory above practice—merely one special path of human civilization, rather than a universal rule?"** When we use one particular path as the sole standard to judge all other civilizations, the "answer" we obtain was already written into the "question" itself.


## IV. A Living Counterexample: Tesla Lost to Edison, but Who Created the Modern World?

If the previous sections dealt with the macro-level paths of nations and eras, then the contrast between Edison and Tesla is a miniature sample condensed into two individuals.

Tesla was the kind of person we habitually call a "genius." He could construct a machine in his mind, run it for weeks, then disassemble it—all without touching a single physical object. He foresaw radio communication, X-rays, remote control, wireless energy transmission, global communication networks—visions dismissed as madness in his lifetime, all realized after his death. He received a complete engineering education and pursued theoretical elegance and perfection.

But he was not good at—or rather, disdained—turning his visions into mass-producible products. After winning the "War of the Currents," Westinghouse Electric fell into financial crisis, and Tesla made a decision later called "unprecedented generosity": he waived the millions of dollars in royalties he was owed on his AC motor patents. He said it was to help the man who had trusted him. The result: he died penniless, spending his final years in a New York hotel, subsisting on feeding sick pigeons on the streets.

Edison was the extreme opposite. He lacked Tesla's theoretical talent and mathematical knowledge; his method was the most rudimentary—trial and error, testing thousands of filament materials. But he understood one thing: **an idea must become a product, enter a factory, and be sold into users' hands to change the world.** His Menlo Park laboratory was the world's first industrial research laboratory; he turned "invention" itself into an organizable, divisible, reproducible process. He built the DC grid, popularized the incandescent bulb, improved the phonograph—within his lifetime, these products tangibly changed ordinary people's lives. He died enormously wealthy, and General Electric became one of the world's largest companies.

Mainstream narratives like to portray Tesla as a "pure genius" and Edison as a "utilitarian businessman." Tesla's image is romantic, solitary, ahead of his time; Edison's is pragmatic, collaborative, even a little ruthless. But this moralistic dichotomy obscures a more important fact: **Tesla's visions took 50 years or more to materialize, while Edison's products put ordinary people to use within five years.**

If a society rewards only "pure theory" and dismisses "engineering execution," it will continuously produce "Teslas"—great visions, destitute lives—without anyone to turn their visions into something ordinary people can reach out and touch. This is a kind of exquisite waste.


## V. A Daily Example: Design Patterns in Software Engineering

If the Industrial Revolution and the two inventors still feel distant to ordinary readers, the software industry offers a more immediate example.

Every programmer learning object-oriented programming has heard of "design patterns"—Singleton, Factory, Observer, Strategy... They are written into textbooks and regarded as "best practice principles" of software design. An inexperienced beginner might easily assume that some theoretician first derived these principles, and then programmers wrote code accordingly.

The truth is exactly the opposite. Design patterns were born from a group of programmers repeatedly discovering across countless real projects that **"doing it this way feels smoother, more stable, and easier to change."** Erich Gamma and others collected, named, and generalized those recurring, proven solutions, eventually publishing the book *Design Patterns* in 1994. The "principles" in the textbook are a summary of existing code practices, not oracles that existed before code was written.

The software industry demonstrates with firsthand experience a more universal law: **knowledge does not proceed from principles to code, but from code to principles.** The best software engineers are often not those with the deepest theory, but those who have written the most code, fallen into the most pits, and accumulated the most "feel" through repeated debugging. Theory has its value, but it is an encoding of experience, not a substitute for it.


## VI. Conclusion: Knowledge Comes from the Soil, Not from the Clouds

Reviewing all the cases above—the blacksmiths and textile workers of the British Industrial Revolution, the factories and production lines of China's reform and opening-up, Edison's industrial laboratory, the design patterns of the software industry—a clear thread emerges:

**Knowledge is first "done," and only then "thought through." Theory is the purification and encoding of practical experience, not its prerequisite.**

This is not to deny the value of theory. Theory is immensely important—it enables experience to be transmitted, taught, and extrapolated into unknown domains. But theory has never been the source of knowledge. The source lies in those hands blackened by machine oil, in that process of trial and error, repeated failure and continuous adjustment, in those improvements that were never written into papers, never awarded degrees, yet genuinely made something faster, cheaper, and more reliable.

We can describe the true movement of knowledge with a revised model:

> **Practice (trial and error, solving concrete problems) → Accumulating local rules → Distilling into systematic theory → Theory feeds back into practice → New practice exposes new problems → Theory iterates again...**

This is a never-closing spiral. Within this spiral, **"production" is both the starting point and the fuel.** It is not that because we have theory, we go produce; rather, it is because we need to produce, need to expand production, need to break through production bottlenecks, that we are compelled to understand the world, distill knowledge, and build theories. Production is not the "downstream application" of theory, but the "native soil" of knowledge.


## Closing Remarks

If this article has a single most important judgment to convey, it is this: **What is mythologized is "pure thought"; what is undervalued is "concrete doing."**

Academia—the very institution that should most honestly reflect on itself—plays a central role in this misjudgment. It enshrines "theory" at the top of the temple and places "technology" and "craft" below; it rewards those who write elegant equations, yet is sparing in its recognition of those who turn those equations into reproducible, low-cost, mass-produced products; it measures the value of knowledge by papers and impact factors, while the silent, collective, iterative cognitive labor on production lines is virtually invisible in its evaluation system.

This is not a harmless preference. This bias is creating a distorted knowledge ecosystem—more and more young people flock to areas where "papers are easy to publish," while fields like process engineering, manufacturing, and applied technology—though critical to national competitiveness—continue to bleed talent and resources. It leads us to mistake the progress of knowledge as primarily emerging from the brainstorming of elite institutions, while ignoring that the breakthroughs that truly changed most people's lives in human history often came first from hands, and only later were understood by minds.

**Knowledge has never been in the sky. It has always grown in the soil—in workshops, on production lines, in the debugging that fails and gets back up again and again. It's just that we haven't looked down for too long.**

And academia today is precisely the place that most needs to lower its head.


## Supplementary Note: On the "Critique of the Needham Question"

After the article was published, some readers asked: "What's the difference between what you're saying and the academic critique of the Needham Question?"

This is a question worth taking seriously. On the surface, my article does seem to participate in the same kind of "cancellation" movement—denying the validity of the Needham Question. But if my position is conflated with certain academic views for that reason, that would be a lazy categorization.

### I. The Standard Academic Critique: Jiang Xiaoyuan's "Pseudo-Problem" Thesis

Professor Jiang Xiaoyuan of Shanghai Jiao Tong University is one of the representative figures in China critiquing the Needham Question. His core argument is:

> The Needham Question is a "pseudo-problem" because it is built on a false premise—the picture of "China's science and technology being world-leading for a long period" is itself a fiction. China and the West followed entirely different paths of technological development; where there is "no one following behind," "leading" is meaningless.

The logic of this argument is: **cancel the premise of comparison, thereby canceling the question itself.** The West took Path A, China took Path B; one cannot say Path A is "ahead of" Path B. If "leading" is invalid, then "why did it later fall behind" becomes a question without a source.

This is a "cancellation-style" critique. It aims to prove that the question should not have been asked in the first place.

### II. My Critique: The Question Itself Exposes the Questioner's Worldview

My article did something different. I did not attempt to prove that "China was once ahead" or "China was not ahead"—that would be fighting on the battlefield set by the opposition. What I did was:

**Examine why the Needham Question is asked at all.**

When someone asks "Why did China not produce modern science?" they have already tacitly assumed the following:

1. "Modern science" = Western modern mathematical-experimental science, the only standard form of human knowledge
2. "Not having it" is a deficiency, a lack, an anomaly that needs to be explained
3. The historical norm is to "produce modern science," and China's path deviated from this norm

In other words, **the question itself uses a Western definition to judge Chinese history.** It appears to be a neutral academic inquiry, but is in fact a culturally-positioned presupposition.

My critique is not "cancellation-style," but "perspectival": **I am not concerned with whether the Needham Question is valid, but with why it is treated as an important question to discuss—what does this "importance" itself reveal?**

### III. The Essence of the Difference

| Dimension | Jiang Xiaoyuan-style Critique | My Critique |
|-----------|-------------------------------|-------------|
| **Target** | The premise of the Needham Question (whether the "leading" narrative holds) | The epistemological presupposition of the question itself (why this is considered a question at all) |
| **Mode of critique** | Cancellation: proving comparison invalid, thereby canceling the question | Perspectival: revealing the worldview behind the question, redefining knowledge |
| **Conclusion** | "Pseudo-question, invalid" | "Pseudo-question, and its pseudo-ness lies not only in method, but in the fact that the entire conception of knowledge is inverted" |
| **Next step** | Stops at negation | Proposes an alternative framework: the practice→theory model of knowledge production |

In one sentence:

**Jiang Xiaoyuan says: "This question was asked incorrectly."**
**What I say is: "The very fact that this question is asked proves that the questioner's understanding of where knowledge comes from is entirely wrong."**

### IV. Why This Is Not the Same Thing

If we liken the Needham Question to a patient, then:

- Jiang Xiaoyuan's diagnosis is: this patient's description of symptoms is wrong, so it's not a real patient and doesn't need treatment
- My diagnosis is: this patient is indeed ill, but the root cause is not in the symptoms it describes—it's that its entire bodily structure's definition of "health" is wrong; and more importantly, the reason it thinks there's a problem here is that it's using the wrong measuring stick

My critique goes further, because it is not simply saying "this question is invalid"—it is saying: **the very fact that this question has been taken seriously for so many years is itself a microcosm of a profound misunderstanding in the history of human knowledge—that misunderstanding being: taking theory as the source of knowledge, and treating production practice as downstream, secondary, manual-labor "application."**

The entire perplexity of the Needham Question stems precisely from this inverted worldview. When we set the conception of knowledge right again—acknowledging that practice precedes theory, that production drives science—this question not only becomes "invalid," but its "invalidity" itself becomes so self-evident as to be unremarkable.

### V. A Brief Additional Note

So if someone equates my critique with the existing academic "pseudo-problem" thesis, I would say: the direction is the same, but the depth is different, and the orientation is different too.

Jiang Xiaoyuan's work is to **cancel an invalid question at the level of historiography.** My work is to **point out, at the level of epistemology, that the very posing of this question is itself a symptom—it is symptomatic of a comprehensive misunderstanding of the sources of knowledge, and the Needham Question is merely one of many symptoms of that misunderstanding.**

I have no intention of belittling the former's work, nor do I intend to place myself in the same camp. After all, the reason I wrote that article was not to cancel a question, but to re-understand the entire world.
